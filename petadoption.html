Objective

Most shelters carry dogs that are “less adoptable”, what can we do to help these dogs find a home faster?

Design Methods

Guerrilla research, Customer journey maps, Personas based on research, Scenarios, Storyboards, Speed dating

I contributed to every step of the process from UX research to our final design solution. I designed our final screens and helped with our filter system.

INTRODUCTION

Designing for the Future of Pets

BACKGROUND

Humane Animal Rescue have rich histories of helping animals in our community. They aim to bring greater efficiency in animal care processes, staff training, and cutting-edge practices to one cohesive organization.

Reasons why we chose Humane Animal Rescue:
1. We identified clear areas of improvement
2. Located in Pittsburgh

The Challenge

How can we support their mission and help sheltered dogs find homes faster?

PRODUCT AT A GLANCE

Humanized Pet Filtering

We designed a filtering system that tailors the results to the user’s favorite qualities about dogs. It also enables users to discover dog breeds that they might not have had in mind in the first place.

DESIGN PROCESS

Our Approach

RESEARCH

Discovering the Needs of Our Stakeholders

Guerrilla Research

We went to the shelter and attended a pet expo to interview adopters, volunteers, employees, and the business owners of shelters. For each, we tailored specific questions to identify the pain points and opportunities. Our observations led us to a few core insights of our key stakeholders:

Customers

People do not have preferences when it came to the physical traits of the dog. They would rather try to find a “connection” with the dog instead.

Service Providers

They strive to get as many animals as they can to be adopted. However, some animals have stereotypes or stigmas- making them less popular and prevents the service provider from achieving this goal.

Business Owners

They are looking for ways to increase animal adoption rates. Ensuring that the adoption process goes well is very crucial in order to increase the retention rates of the new family pet.

PROBLEM

Adoption Barriers

We found that people adopt dogs mostly based on how well they connect. However, many senior dogs and black dogs are overlooked before getting to the meeting stage. This is the main service provider problem we wanted to focus on. We scoped down from the entire process that a dog goes through in the shelter, which included getting noticed on social media and potentially being given up again after adoption.

ANALYSIS

Customer Journey Mapping

Keeping the stakeholder triangle in mind, we synthesized our research into insights, pain points, and opportunities.

Adopter's Journey Map

During our first critique, we were told that this was too broad and to focus of one aspect so we decided to focus of the period of time before adopting a dog.

Service Providers's Journey Map

By analyzing the time before a dog was adopted, We learned that the only interaction between customer and service provider was the pre-adoption and adoption phases.

Creating Useful Personas

Creating the right persona influenced our direction for design. We knew it was critical to base our persona on our research with real people and not based on assumptions. The key goal for Jhené was to focus on getting her a dog that matches her existing dog.

Developing Our Scenarios and Storyboards

After writing out 20 scenarios, we narrowed down into 8 different scenarios to storyboard. Practicing getting our ideas out helped us get rid of bad ideas faster as well as allow us to think creatively about our potential solutions. Our main takeaway from generating many concepts was to help us discover new possibilities and opportunities that are there as value for our stakeholders.

INSIGHTS

People Care About Personality

After analyzing the feedback we got from speed dating we were able to gain several insights:

1. People have preferences on the characteristics of dogs.
2. People are not as interested in exposure to detailed information on dogs initially.
3. They want a smooth adoption process.
4. They want to learn more about the qualities of the dogs.

GOALS

Design Decisions

LOW-FI PROTOTYPING

Sketching and Wireframes

We quickly sketched out our design and screen map to make sure everyone’s on the same page.

We then produced low-fi wireframes in Balsamiq for each of the screens we were designing.

First Critque Feedback

Feedback on this phase was focused on the scope of our proposed features, causing us to narrow our focus further and shed portions of the site that didn’t explicitly need to be represented or redesigned. Additionally, we cut many of the service provider specific features included in these wireframes to focus on meeting the users’ goals first, with the understanding the better pet-adopter matches would satisfy the underlying needs of the service provider as well.

MID-FI PROTOTYPING

Screen Mapping and Mid-Fi Wireframes

We created a clearer site map and focused on creating screens that were being innovated instead of focusing on every screen.

In contrast to prior screens, the results display does a good job of withholding the more technical or medical information associated with a pet until you click into their profile page, only displaying an image, their name, and few select tags taken from the existing biography information. This allows users to connect with dogs visually and get a sense of their personality, both things that potential pet owners do naturally upon meeting a dog for the first time.

Second Critque Feedback

The feedback from this crit is what allowed us to heavily scope down to one feature to redesign for maximum user impact and stakeholder benefit. We needed to translate the personal needs of the user into standard language that service providers at Humane Animal Rescue could use to best match potential adoptees with pets in need.

We critically reviewed the sum of our research and efforts thus far and refocused on this new goal of humanizing the rigid, cold, and callous process of browsing a database of adoptable pets online. With this and mind, we began on the next phase of the assignment focusing on understanding the user in human terms, posing questions in natural phrases and language, and highlighting the underlying empathy in the pet adoption process.

FINAL DESIGN

Hi-Fidelity Wireframing and Prototyping

Based on all discussions we had and feedback we got, our objective was scoped down to helping first time adopters adopt what are generally less adopted, such as senior or black dogs. We did not only scope our topic, we also majorly scoped down our focus on the website. We went from wanting to include numerous features in our website to just focusing on our filter function.

The problem space we focused on in the end was creating a filter function that helps first time adopters find their ideal dog while promoting the dogs that are less likely to be adopted in shelters.

Final Screen Map
Main Filter Flow

After our first user testing, we realized we needed to create more feedback in terms of typography, color, and spacing. We needed to occupy more screen space for the filter, and allowing the user to go in-and-out of the filter, rather that scrolling all the way back up. We also wanted to create a more “fun” aspect to filtering by using a paw, small micro-interaction that user might find rewarding.

We knew we did not want to let the filter hinder the results of other potential dogs so we used a toggle switch to allow the user to afford the on/off states of the filter.

Typography & Color Palette

Upon our team’s decision to keep the app simple but vibrant, I chose a welcoming and optimistic typeface that paired well with our interface.

To have that vibrant, engaging look, I chose the color poppy red and keeping other colors neutral to increase visual interest.

Changes I made:
  • Increased readability and visibility with color
  • Better use of space and padding
  • Clearer titles and increased visual feedback to the user
  • Added icons for each filter option

The new design aims to give the user increased feedback with their decisions by utilizing information architecture and hierarchy.

NEXT STEPS

My Final Thoughts

It was easy to rush into designing what we wanted to achieve, which was a system that could help less adoptable dogs get adopted. However, I quickly learned that it is not that easy and that there are many design considerations that I did not even think of.

  • A pivotal moment in this project was when I learned that too much information gathering would hinder the user from continuing with the filter, creating a barrier to entry.
  • Learning to put the more humanized filter first, before all the general information, made me realize how important progressive disclosure is in order to develop trust in the design.
  • It was especially hard to think of design in the light of three different stakeholders. It really pushed me to think beyond just the user.
  • This was my favorite project this semester, not just because our topic was on pet adoption, but because there was so much more to think and ponder about.
